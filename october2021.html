October 2021

Getting Back To Work

As I started to adjust to having back in-person school I started to grasp what projects I would like to work on for the upcoming academic school year. Now that I have a new and more capable laptop I am able to explore more with different software and projects without the worry of storage or RAM. I decided that one of the projects I’ve always been fascinated with is Generative Art.

Generative Art

Generative Art has perplexed me for years especially since it includes some of my biggest interests, such as art and programming. This was practically created just for me! This goal also coincides with the art course I am currently enrolled in, Art Special Projects. Which is an art course in which you have the freedom to choose and work on any project within the arts that suits you and your interest. So, for my first project, I chose Generative Art. So it was time to stop stalling and explore what the vast world has to offer! So far I have learned a lot regarding not only Generative Art but art concepts and fundamentals.

The tools I researched for this project included P5.JS (a free JavaScript library) and Processing (a free graphical library). For the project so far I have experimented with P5.JS the most, mostly due to the ease of access and resources relating to the program.

Before even diving into any programming and developing art pieces I had to first learn about the concepts. This is not always the most fun part (trust me I know), but it is a necessity to truly understand the depth of what goes into a piece. Regarding concepts this month, I learned about color theory and the type of color palettes.

I’ve heard about color theory here and there but I never truly understood the concept of it. During my research, I discovered that color theory in regards to generative art is the way of adding some mathematical equations or symbols to the ways colors get used and mixed in an art place. There are 2 main color models: additive and subtractive colors. Additive color models are used for mixing color lights. For example, if a red and green light is shined on the same spot, that spot will appear yellow. The other color model is subtractive colors which are for mixing pigments. If a blue and yellow pigment are mixed, then green would appear.

Also, I learned about the types of color palettes: monochromatic, complementary, triadic, analogous, split complementary, and tetradic. A monochromatic color palette is commonly used to do a single color print. For this, the artist would pick the same value of the hue and change the brightness or saturation of that color. A complementary color palette is when an artist uses 2 colors that are on the opposite side of the color wheel. This color palette is typically used for creating very striking color palettes that have extremely high contrast and are mostly seen as sports team colors. One of if not the most complex color palettes is the triadic color palette which its name states uses 3 equidistant colors on the color wheel. Particularly useful for interior decorating. On the contrary, there is the analogous color palette that takes colors from the same side of the color wheel and comes across as low contrast and serene. Next is the split complementary color palette which is a mixture of triadic and complementary. Lastly, is the tetradic color palette that is also pretty complex and takes complementary colors and goes out by one on either side.

So in conclusion ...

So, in conclusion, I’ve adjusted to in-person courses and started back working on a new project, learned about color theory, color palettes, color models and I intend to pursue my studies more in this vast abstract world that is Generative Art and other fields.
